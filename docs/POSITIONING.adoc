== Core Positioning

**Groove is not a replacement for anything. It is the thing that goes between
the things you already have.**

Your services already work alone. Groove makes them work together — without
integration code, without configuration, without either service knowing the
other exists in advance. Start one. Start the other. They find each other.
Extra features appear. Stop one. The other keeps working.

That's it. That's Groove.

== What Groove Is NOT (And Who It Does Not Compete With)

[cols="2,3,3"]
|===
| Technology | What it does | Why Groove is different

| **Service meshes** (Istio, Linkerd)
| Manage traffic between services: routing, load balancing, observability
| Groove doesn't manage traffic. It manages _capability composition_. You
  still need a service mesh if you need traffic management. Groove sits above
  that layer — it's about what services _can do together_, not how packets
  flow between them.

| **Plugin systems** (VS Code, WordPress, Obsidian)
| Extend a host application with subordinate extensions
| Groove has no host. Both sides are peers. Neither is primary, neither is
  subordinate. A guitar plugged into an amp isn't a "guitar plugin" — they're
  two independent instruments that compose.

| **API gateways** (Kong, Traefik)
| Proxy and manage API traffic
| Groove is not a proxy. Systems connect directly. No intermediary sits
  between them.

| **Message brokers** (Kafka, RabbitMQ)
| Store and deliver messages between producers and consumers
| Groove doesn't store or deliver messages. When it needs async events, it
  uses BEAM's built-in PubSub — zero external dependencies. Groove is about
  _what fits_, not _what flows_.

| **gRPC / REST / GraphQL**
| Transport protocols for sending data between services
| Groove is not a transport protocol. It uses HTTP for discovery, but the
  groove itself is a _structural relationship_ between type-safe interfaces.
  You can use gRPC as the transport inside a grooved connection.

| **Docker Compose / Kubernetes**
| Orchestrate containers
| Groove doesn't orchestrate. It doesn't start, stop, or schedule anything.
  It discovers what's already running and composes with it.
|===

**The positioning is: Groove occupies unclaimed space.** No existing tool does
what Groove does. There is nothing to switch from.

== Who Groove Serves

=== The Lost: "I don't know what I need"

These people have independent services that _should_ work together but don't.
They've tried:

- Hard-coding API calls between services (works until one changes)
- Writing integration middleware (becomes its own maintenance burden)
- Using an API gateway (adds complexity, doesn't solve composition)
- Giving up and accepting silos

**What Groove gives them:** Start your services. They find each other. Done.
No middleware to write, no gateway to configure, no API calls to maintain.
The integration you didn't have to write.

**Entry message:** _"Your services already work alone. What if they worked
together — without you writing the glue?"_

=== The Frustrated: "I know what I need but nothing gives it to me"

These people have specific pain:

[cols="2,3"]
|===
| Pain | What Groove solves

| "Every time we update Service A, Service B breaks"
| Groove's type system proves compatibility at compile time. If the types
  don't fit, the connection cannot be made. You find out before deployment,
  not in production at 3am.

| "We spend 40% of our time writing integration code"
| Groove eliminates integration code. Services declare what they offer and
  what they consume. If the shapes match, they compose. No glue needed.

| "Adding voice/chat/storage to our app means becoming experts in those
  domains"
| Groove's plug-and-play model means you start Burble alongside your app
  and voice appears. You didn't become a voice company. You just plugged
  into the groove.

| "Our plugin system is fragile — plugins crash the host, conflict with
  each other, and break on updates"
| Groove has no host. Both sides are peers. Linear types guarantee that
  connections are properly closed. Capability types guarantee that only
  compatible systems connect. No crashes, no conflicts.

| "I self-host 12 services and wish they'd just discover each other"
| That's literally what Groove does. Start them. They find each other.
  Stop one. The others keep working.

| "We need formal verification but our integration layer is unverifiable"
| Groove's Idris2 ABI layer is formally verified with dependent types.
  Proofs — not tests — guarantee correctness.
|===

**Entry message:** _"You've been writing integration code because nothing
else would. Groove is the integration you don't have to maintain."_

== Positioning Channels

=== Channel 1: Self-Hosters and Homelab Communities

**Who:** People running Nextcloud, Jellyfin, Home Assistant, Gitea, etc. on
their own hardware.

**Pain:** Services run side-by-side but are islands. Adding integrations means
editing config files, setting up webhooks, or installing connectors.

**Groove message:** _"Run your services. They find each other. Extra features
appear."_

**Entry point:** `groove-manifest` (Level 1). Just add a manifest endpoint
to your service. 30 minutes. No dependency on Idris2 — just an A2ML endpoint
over HTTP.

**Amplifier:** A blog post: _"How I got my self-hosted services to discover
each other in 30 minutes without writing a single integration."_

=== Channel 2: Formally Verified Systems Communities

**Who:** People in aerospace, medical, automotive, financial — domains where
"it works on my machine" isn't good enough.

**Pain:** They have formally verified components but the _integration between
components_ is unverified glue code. The proofs stop at the boundary.

**Groove message:** _"The integration layer itself is formally verified.
The Idris2 dependent types prove that if A connects to B, the composition
is sound."_

**Entry point:** `groove-proven` (Level 3). Show them the Idris2 proofs.

**Amplifier:** An arXiv paper: _"Groove: Formally Verified System Composition
via Dependent Types and Linear Resources."_

=== Channel 3: Desktop Application Developers

**Who:** People building desktop apps who want optional features (voice, chat,
storage, integrity verification) without baking them in.

**Pain:** Either they build everything monolithically or they build a plugin
system (which is its own maintenance nightmare).

**Groove message:** _"Your app works alone. Start Burble alongside it and
voice appears. Start VeriSimDB and persistence appears. Stop them and your
app keeps working. No plugins, no configuration."_

**Entry point:** Gossamer as a working example. Show the combination matrix.

**Amplifier:** A demo video: _"Watch what happens when I start Burble
alongside Gossamer."_ (30-second demo — voice UI appears, stop Burble,
voice UI disappears, app keeps working.)

=== Channel 4: Frustrated Microservice Teams

**Who:** Teams with 20+ microservices who spend more time on integration than
features.

**Pain:** Every new service means writing API clients, updating gateways,
configuring service mesh rules, and hoping nothing breaks.

**Groove message:** _"What if your services just told each other what they
can do, and composed automatically?"_

**Entry point:** `groove-manifest` + `groove-lifecycle` (Level 2). Add
to one service, then another. Watch them find each other.

**Amplifier:** A case study: _"We replaced 3,000 lines of integration code
with 50 lines of Groove manifests."_

=== Channel 5: Language and Runtime Communities

**Who:** Elixir/BEAM developers, Rust systems programmers, Zig embedded
developers, Idris2/dependent-type researchers.

**Pain varies by community:**

- **Elixir:** "We have great distributed primitives but no standard way
  to compose with non-BEAM systems."
- **Rust:** "We have great safety but no standard composition model."
- **Zig:** "We can call anything but there's no discovery layer."
- **Idris2:** "We have dependent types but no real-world integration
  framework that uses them."

**Groove message:** Tailored per community. For Elixir: _"Groove uses BEAM
clustering for distributed discovery — it's native to your runtime."_ For
Rust: _"Groove's Zig FFI layer means zero overhead from Rust — just call
the C functions."_ For Idris2: _"Groove is dependent types applied to
real-world system composition."_

**Entry point:** Language-specific binding generators (V API layer).

== Positioning Sequence

The order matters. Start where Groove has the least competition and the most
obvious value.

[cols="1,2,3"]
|===
| Phase | Audience | Action

| **Phase 1**
| Self-hosters, homelab, indie devs
| Blog posts, demo videos, "30-minute Groove" tutorial. Show the magic
  moment: start two services, they find each other, features appear.
  _No theory. Just the experience._

| **Phase 2**
| Desktop app developers
| Gossamer + Burble demo. Combination matrix. Show the Groove pattern
  working in a real app people can download and try.

| **Phase 3**
| Formally verified systems
| arXiv paper. Conference talk. Show the Idris2 proofs. Target ICFP, POPL,
  or a dependent types workshop. _This is where the academic credibility
  comes from._

| **Phase 4**
| Microservice teams
| Case studies. Integration code reduction metrics. Show the before/after.
  _This is where enterprise adoption starts._

| **Phase 5**
| Language communities
| Language-specific binding packages. Talks at ElixirConf, RustConf,
  Zig meetups. _"Here's how Groove works in your language."_
|===

== What NOT To Do

. **Don't position against service meshes.** They solve a different problem.
  Groove is complementary. A service mesh manages traffic; Groove manages
  composition. Both can coexist.

. **Don't lead with Idris2.** Lead with the experience. "Your services
  find each other" is compelling. "We use Idris2 dependent types" is
  alienating to most developers. The types are the engine, not the paint
  job. Show the paint job first.

. **Don't promise to replace anything.** Groove adds a layer that didn't
  exist. It doesn't replace REST, gRPC, Kubernetes, or anything else.
  _"It's the thing that goes between the things you already have."_

. **Don't launch with all four conformance levels.** Launch Level 1
  (`groove-manifest`) only. Let people add the manifest to their service,
  see the magic, and then ask "how do I get type safety?" That's when you
  introduce Level 3.

. **Don't write a manifesto.** Write a tutorial. The first experience
  should be: install, add 10 lines, start two services, see them find
  each other. Under 5 minutes. The philosophy comes after the experience.

== Success Metrics

Groove's positioning is working if:

. People try Level 1 without being told to (`groove-manifest` adoption)
. Nobody asks "what does this replace?" (positioning is clear)
. People ask "how do I get the type safety?" (pull, not push)
. Blog posts and talks reference Groove as "complementary to" other tools,
  not "competing with" them
. The self-hosted community adopts the `.well-known/groove` convention
  independently

== Final Thought

Groove's greatest positioning asset is that it doesn't fight anyone.
It fills a gap nobody knew they had. The moment someone sees two services
discover each other without configuration, they understand. Everything
after that — the types, the proofs, the mesh, the attestation — is just
"yes, and."

Lead with the magic moment. The theory can wait.
